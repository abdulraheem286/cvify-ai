A CV personal statement is a short paragraph at the top of your CV, usually 3 to 4 sentences, that sums up who you are, what you are good at, and the role you want. Think of it as a quick pitch: it tells the reader why they should keep reading. Get it right and the rest of your CV gets a fair look.

This guide shows you exactly what to put in a CV personal statement, how long it should be, and how to tailor it. You will also find short examples for a graduate, a career changer, and an experienced professional that you can copy the shape of.

## What is a CV personal statement for?

Recruiters often skim the top of your CV first. Your personal statement is the first thing they read, so it decides whether they keep going or move on. A good one answers three questions fast:

- **Who are you?** Your job title or field (for example, "marketing graduate" or "registered nurse").
- **What are you good at?** One or two strengths that matter for this job.
- **What do you want?** The role or type of work you are aiming for.

If a stranger read only this paragraph, they should understand your value in a few seconds. That is the whole job.

## How long should a CV personal statement be?

Short. Aim for about 50 to 100 words, or 3 to 4 sentences. It should sit in one tidy paragraph, not a wall of text.

Longer is not better. If your statement runs past four sentences, it stops being a hook and starts being a chore to read. Cut anything that does not add proof or direction.

## What to include (and what to leave out)

Include:

- Your professional identity (title, field, or years of experience).
- One or two key skills that match the job advert.
- A real result or strength, with a number if you have one.
- The role or goal you are aiming for.

Leave out:

- Tired clichés like "hardworking team player" or "results-driven professional". They tell the reader nothing.
- Vague claims you cannot back up, such as "excellent communicator", with no example.
- Personal details like age, marital status, or hobbies that do not help.
- The same generic statement copied onto every application.

The last point matters most. A personal statement written for one specific job will always beat a one-size-fits-all version. Read the advert, note the words they use, and mirror the ones that are true for you.

## How do you write a CV personal statement step by step?

1. **Write your CV first, then the statement last.** Once the rest is done, you know what to highlight.
2. **Start with your identity.** "Final-year computer science student..." or "Customer service manager with 8 years' experience...".
3. **Add one or two proof points.** A skill plus evidence beats a skill on its own.
4. **End with your goal.** Name the role or the kind of team you want to join.
5. **Trim it.** Read it out loud. Cut filler words until every sentence earns its place.

## CV personal statement examples

Below are three short examples for different situations. Notice how each one names a role, shows a strength, and points at a goal. Copy the structure, not the exact words.

### Example: recent graduate

> Final-year marketing graduate with hands-on experience from a six-month internship at a retail brand, where I helped grow the brand's Instagram following by 30%. Confident with content planning, basic analytics, and paid social. Looking for a junior marketing role where I can keep learning and take ownership of real campaigns.

Why it works: a graduate has little job history, so this leans on a placement, a real number, and clear enthusiasm.

### Example: career changer

> Former secondary school teacher moving into UX design, with a completed design certificate and three portfolio projects built for real local businesses. Skilled at understanding people, explaining complex ideas simply, and testing what actually works. Seeking a junior UX role on a team that values curiosity and clear communication.

Why it works: a career changer must connect old skills to new ones. This names the switch openly, then shows transferable strengths and proof of new training.

### Example: experienced professional

> Registered nurse with 10 years' experience across A&E and intensive care, known for staying calm under pressure and mentoring junior staff. Led a ward handover process that cut medication errors by 15%. Now seeking a senior nursing role where I can shape patient care and support a growing team.

Why it works: experience is the selling point here, so it leads with years, a measurable result, and a step up in responsibility.

## A quick before-and-after

Small changes make a big difference. Compare these two:

> **Before:** "Hardworking and motivated individual seeking a new opportunity to use my skills and grow within a great company."

> **After:** "Detail-focused accounts assistant with 3 years' experience and a 99% invoice accuracy record. Comfortable with Xero and month-end reporting. Looking to join a finance team where I can take on more responsibility."

The "before" version could belong to anyone. The "after" version names a role, gives proof, and shows direction. That is the difference between skimmed and shortlisted.

## Key takeaways

- A CV personal statement is a 3 to 4 sentence paragraph at the top of your CV that pitches who you are, what you do well, and the role you want.
- Keep it to roughly 50 to 100 words and write it last, once the rest of your CV is done.
- Include your identity, one or two proof points (numbers help), and a clear goal.
- Tailor it to each job and cut clichés like "hardworking team player".
- Pick first or third person and stay consistent throughout.
