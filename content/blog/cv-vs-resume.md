People often use the words "CV" and "resume" as if they mean the same thing. Sometimes they do, and sometimes they do not. This guide explains the CV vs resume question in plain language, so you know exactly which document to send and when.

## What Is a CV?

CV stands for "curriculum vitae." It is Latin for "course of life."

A CV is a detailed record of your career. It lists your full history: your education, work experience, skills, and often extra sections like publications, research, awards, and conference talks.

A true CV has a few key traits:

- It is long. It can run two pages or many more.
- It grows over time. You add to it as your career moves forward.
- It stays fairly complete. You do not usually cut things out to save space.

## What Is a Resume?

A resume is a short summary of your skills and experience. The word comes from French and means "summary." That is the whole idea.

A resume is built for one goal: to get you an interview for a specific job. So it is focused and tight.

A resume usually has these traits:

- It is short. Most are one page, sometimes two.
- It is tailored. You change it to match each job you apply for.
- It is selective. You only include what matters for that role.

## CV vs Resume: The Key Differences

Here is the CV vs resume comparison at a glance.

| Feature | CV | Resume |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Length | Two or more pages | One page (sometimes two) |
| Detail | Full career history | Only relevant highlights |
| Purpose | A complete record of your work | A pitch for one specific job |
| Changes | Grows over time, rarely trimmed | Tailored for each application |
| Common sections | Publications, research, awards | Work experience, skills, education |

The short version: a CV tells your whole story. A resume tells the part of the story that fits the job in front of you.

## Regional Differences

This is where it gets confusing. The same word can mean different things depending on where you live.

**United States and Canada**

- "Resume" is the standard word for a short, one-page job document.
- "CV" is used mainly in academia, science, and medicine. In these fields, a CV is the long, detailed document described above.

**United Kingdom, Ireland, and much of Europe**

- "CV" is the everyday word for a job application document.
- Here, a "CV" is usually short, like a US resume. Most people never use the word "resume" at all.

**Australia and New Zealand**

- "CV" and "resume" are often used to mean the same thing: a short job document.

**Academia (anywhere)**

- If you apply for a research, teaching, or PhD role, "CV" almost always means the long, full-history version, no matter the country.

So before you apply, check what the employer expects. When a UK job ad asks for a "CV," it usually wants a short, focused document, not a ten-page history.

## When to Use Which

Use a **resume** (or a short CV) when:

- You apply for most jobs in business, tech, retail, trades, or healthcare support roles.
- The employer asks for a one-page or short document.
- You are in the US or Canada and applying outside academia.

Use a **full CV** when:

- You apply for academic, research, or scientific roles.
- You need to list publications, grants, or teaching history.
- A university, journal, or grant application asks for one.

## Which Should You Use? A Quick Guide

Not sure? Walk through these questions.

1. **What does the job ad ask for?** Use the exact word the employer uses. This is the most reliable clue.
2. **Where is the job?** In the US or Canada, "resume" usually means short. In the UK and Europe, "CV" usually means short.
3. **Is it an academic or research role?** If yes, use a full, detailed CV.
4. **When in doubt, keep it short.** For most jobs, a clear one or two page document beats a long one. You can always send more detail if asked.
