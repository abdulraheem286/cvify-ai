To write a cover letter, open with the exact role you want, give one or two short paragraphs of proof that you can do the job, and close with a clear next step. It is a one-page note that connects your CV to a specific employer.

Think of it as the human voice next to your CV. Your CV lists the facts; the cover letter tells the reader why those facts matter for this role. Below is the structure, step by step, with short examples you can copy the shape of.

## The four parts of a cover letter

Every strong cover letter has the same four parts, in this order:

1. **Header and contact details** — your name, contact info, and the date.
2. **Greeting and opening** — who you are writing to and which job you want.
3. **Body paragraphs** — one or two paragraphs proving you can do the work.
4. **Closing** — a polite sign-off with a clear call to action.

Follow those four steps and you will never stare at a blank page again. Let's take them one at a time.

## Step 1: Header and contact details

Start with a small, clean header. Include your full name, phone number, professional email, and city. Add a link to your LinkedIn or portfolio if it helps.

If you are sending a formal letter (not an email or an online form), add the date and, when you know it, the hiring manager's name, job title, and company. In an email, you can skip most of this and let your email signature do the work.

Keep this block short. It is signposting, not the main event.

## Step 2: Greeting and opening

Address a real person whenever you can. "Dear Ms. Khan" beats "Dear Hiring Manager", and both beat "To whom it may concern". Check the job post, the company website, or LinkedIn to find a name. If you truly cannot, "Dear Hiring Team" is a safe fallback.

Your opening line is the most important sentence in the letter. Do not waste it on "I am writing to apply for..." The reader already knows that. Instead, name the role and give one specific reason you fit or one thing you genuinely rate about the company.

Here is a short opening you can adapt:

> Dear Ms. Khan,
>
> I'm applying for the Customer Support Lead role at Brightline. Over the past four years I've cut support response times and trained new agents, and your focus on fast, human help is exactly the kind of team I want to build with.

Notice it names the role, the company, a result, and a reason. That is a lot of signal in three sentences.

## Step 3: Body paragraphs

This is where you prove it. Use one or two short paragraphs to connect your experience to what the job actually needs.

The trick is to pick two or three requirements from the job post and match each one to real proof from your work. Numbers help: a percentage, a time saved, a count. This is the same detail work that powers a strong CV, so the skills and achievements you gathered for your CV feed straight into your cover letter.

Follow this simple pattern for the body:

- Pick two or three things the job clearly needs.
- For each, give a specific example of when you did it.
- Add a result or number wherever you honestly can.
- Tie it back to what this employer is trying to achieve.

Do not just repeat your CV line by line. The CV is the full list; the cover letter is the highlight reel with a story around it. Explain the "why" and the "so what" that bullet points cannot.

If you are changing careers or have a gap, the body is the place to address it briefly and confidently, then pivot back to what you bring.

## Step 4: The closing

End with a short, warm paragraph. Thank the reader, restate your interest in one line, and add a clear call to action, usually that you would welcome the chance to talk.

Here is a closing you can adapt:

> I'd love to show how I can help Brightline keep support fast as you grow. Thank you for reading, and I'd welcome a conversation whenever suits you.
>
> Best regards,
> Amina Rahman

Then sign off with "Best regards" or "Kind regards" and your name. Simple and professional wins.

## Do's and don'ts

A quick checklist to keep your letter sharp:

- **Do** tailor every letter to the specific role and company.
- **Do** address a named person when you can find one.
- **Do** lead with results and real examples, not adjectives.
- **Do** keep it to one page, three or four short paragraphs.
- **Do** proofread, then read it aloud to catch clumsy lines.
- **Don't** copy your CV word for word.
- **Don't** open with "I am writing to apply for the position of..."
- **Don't** use one generic letter for every job.
- **Don't** talk only about what you want; focus on what you offer.
- **Don't** exaggerate or claim skills you cannot back up.

## Key takeaways

- A cover letter has four parts: header, opening, body, and closing.
- Your opening line should name the role and one specific reason you fit.
- Use the body to prove two or three job requirements with real examples and numbers.
- Keep it to one page and tailor it to each employer; never send a generic letter.
- Your CV's achievements and skills are the raw material for a strong cover letter, so build the CV first.
