To tailor your CV, match the keywords in the job description and highlight the most relevant experience for each role you apply to. In plain terms, you are reshaping the same real facts about your career so the parts that matter most for one specific job sit right at the top. Do this well and you help both the software and the human on the other side see you as a strong fit fast.

## Why Tailoring Your CV Works

Most CVs are sent out the same way to every job. That is a mistake. A hiring team looks for someone who fits *their* role, not a general good candidate.

There are two readers you need to win over. The first is an ATS (applicant tracking system), which is software that scans your CV, pulls out details like skills and job titles, and stores them so recruiters can search. The second is the human recruiter who reads what the software passes along. When you tailor your CV, you speak to both. You use the words the software is searching for, and you show the human your best-matched experience without making them dig.

## How to Tailor Your CV: A Step-by-Step Process

Follow these five steps for each job you apply to.

1. **Read the posting closely.** Go through the full job description twice. The first read is for the big picture. The second is to spot the skills, tools, and duties the employer repeats or lists first. Repeated words are clues to what they care about most.
2. **Extract the key skills and keywords.** Make a short list of the exact terms in the posting. Include hard skills (like "Excel," "SEO," or "project management"), tools, and the job title itself. These are your target keywords.
3. **Mirror the employer's language.** Where your experience is a true match, use their exact wording. If the posting says "stakeholder management," use that phrase instead of a synonym like "working with clients." The ATS often searches for exact terms, so matching the wording helps.
4. **Reorder for relevance.** Move your most relevant jobs, bullet points, and skills to the top. Recruiters skim. Put the strongest match where their eyes land first, and trim or shorten the parts that do not fit this role.
5. **Quantify your results.** Add numbers wherever you can. "Grew sign-ups by 30% in six months" is far stronger than "responsible for growth." Numbers make your claims real and easy to trust.

## What to Adjust (and What to Leave Alone)

You do not rewrite your whole CV each time. You keep one strong master version and adjust a few key parts. Here is what to change for each application:

- **The professional summary.** Rewrite these two or three lines to name the role and your best-matched strengths.
- **The skills section.** Reorder it so the skills named in the posting come first. Remove skills that are not relevant to this job.
- **The top bullet points.** Under each recent role, put the achievements that match the job description at the top.
- **The keywords.** Weave the employer's exact terms into your experience naturally, only where they are true for you.
- **The job title match.** If your past title was close to the target role, you can add a plain, honest label that reflects the work you actually did.

Leave the core facts alone. Your dates, employers, and real achievements stay the same. Tailoring is about emphasis, not invention.

## A Quick Before and After

Say the job asks for someone to "manage social media campaigns and report on performance." Here is a weak line next to a tailored one:

> Before: "Helped with the company's social media."
> After: "Managed 4 social media campaigns and reported monthly on performance, lifting engagement by 22%."

Same job, same person. The second line mirrors the posting's language, adds numbers, and shows a clear result. That is tailoring in one sentence.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even careful applicants slip up. Watch for these:

- **Keyword stuffing.** Do not paste a wall of skills or repeat the same term over and over. It reads as spam to humans and can hurt you.
- **Copying the posting word for word.** Mirror key terms, but write in your own voice. A CV that echoes the whole ad looks lazy.
- **Forgetting to update the summary.** The top of your CV is prime space. Leaving a generic summary wastes it.
- **Claiming skills you do not have.** Matching the language is smart. Faking experience is not, and it tends to surface in interviews.

## Key Takeaways

- Tailor your CV by matching the job's keywords and moving your most relevant experience to the top.
- Read the posting twice, extract its key skills, and mirror the exact wording where it is true for you.
- Keep one master CV and adjust the summary, skills, and top bullets for each job instead of starting over.
- Add numbers to your results so your claims are clear and believable.
- Tailoring is honest emphasis, not invention. Only claim skills and results you actually have.
