To write a CV with no experience, lead with your education, then fill the rest with projects, volunteering, part-time or casual work, and transferable skills like communication, teamwork, and reliability. You do not need a job history to prove you are capable. You just need to show effort and results in whatever you have already done.

Everyone starts somewhere, and a strong first CV is about presentation, not padding. This guide walks you through what to include and how to make ordinary experiences sound like real achievements.

## Start with what you do have

When you have no paid work history, the trick is to widen your definition of "experience." A school project, a fundraiser you helped run, a weekend babysitting job, a personal website you built for fun, all of these show skills an employer cares about. Your job is to notice them and describe them clearly.

Here is a simple order that works well for a CV with no experience:

- **Contact details** — full name, phone number, professional email, and city.
- **Personal statement** — two or three sentences on who you are and what you want.
- **Education** — your strongest asset, so it goes near the top.
- **Projects** — school, coursework, or things you built on your own.
- **Volunteering and activities** — clubs, societies, events, community work.
- **Part-time or casual work** — any job, even short or informal ones.
- **Skills** — the practical abilities that match the role.
- **Extras** — certifications, languages, or awards.

## Put education front and centre

With little work history, your education does the heavy lifting, so give it room. List your most recent or highest qualification first. Include the course or school name, and the year you finished (or "expected 2027" if you are still studying).

New students and graduates can add more here than an experienced worker would. Include relevant subjects or modules, a strong grade, a project or thesis topic, and any academic award. If a module connects directly to the job, name it. This is where you show what you have been learning and doing.

## Use projects to prove your skills

Projects are one of the best ways to fill a CV with no experience, because they show you can actually do something, not just talk about it. A project can be:

- A coursework assignment or group project from school.
- Something you built for yourself (a blog, an app, a design, a small business).
- A challenge or competition you took part in.

Describe each one the way you would describe a job: what you set out to do, what you did, and what came of it. A personal project you cared about often says more about you than a job title ever could.

## Count volunteering and part-time work

Volunteering counts as real experience. Helping at a charity event, coaching a junior team, or running a school society all show responsibility and teamwork. So does part-time or casual work, whether it is retail, hospitality, tutoring, babysitting, or a paper round. None of these are "too small" for a first CV.

The key is to turn a plain duty into an achievement. A **duty** is what you were meant to do. An **achievement** is what actually happened, ideally with a number or result. Watch an ordinary task become something an employer notices:

> **Before:** "Helped out at the school charity bake sale."

> **After:** "Organised a school bake sale with 5 classmates, raising £320 for charity in one afternoon."

The "after" version names what you did, who you worked with, and the result. You do not need exact figures. Estimates, rough counts, and "about" numbers all count.

## Highlight transferable skills

Transferable skills are abilities that carry from one setting to another. You have built plenty already, even without a job. Think about:

- **Communication** — presentations, group work, customer chats.
- **Teamwork** — sports, clubs, group projects.
- **Reliability and time management** — juggling study, deadlines, and commitments.
- **Problem solving** — fixing a project that went wrong, learning a new tool.
- **Digital skills** — software, spreadsheets, design tools, or coding.

List the ones that match the job, and be honest, only add skills you could talk about in an interview. For a fuller list to draw from, see the [best skills to put on a CV](/blog/best-skills-for-cv-2026).

## Write a short personal statement

A personal statement is two or three sentences at the top of your CV. It tells the reader who you are, what you are good at, and what you are aiming for. When you have no job history, this helps the reader place you straight away.

Keep it specific and forward-looking. Instead of "Hardworking person looking for any opportunity," try something like: "Business student with strong organisation and teamwork skills, gained through group projects and volunteering. Looking for a first role in marketing where I can learn and contribute." It names who you are, a couple of strengths, and a clear goal.

## Keep it to one page

A CV with no experience should be one page. You have less to cover, so a tight, clean page reads better than a stretched-out two pages. Use clear headings, short bullets, and plenty of white space. Send it as a PDF so your layout stays put, and pick a plain, readable font.

Do not pad it with filler or list skills you cannot back up. A focused one-pager that tells a clear story beats a long one full of vague claims.

## Key takeaways

- **Lead with education**, then projects, volunteering, part-time work, and transferable skills.
- **Redefine experience** — coursework, personal projects, clubs, and casual jobs all count.
- **Turn duties into achievements** by adding a result or rough number wherever you can.
- **Add a short personal statement** so the reader can place you quickly.
- **Keep it to one clean page** and save it as a PDF.

Writing a CV with no experience is far more doable than it feels. Gather what you have, describe it in terms of results, and shape everything around the role you want.
