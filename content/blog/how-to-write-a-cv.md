A good CV (curriculum vitae) is simply a clear summary of your work, skills, and results. It is the document that gets you the interview, so every line should earn its place. This guide shows you how to write a CV from scratch, section by section, with short examples you can copy the shape of.

## What goes on a CV (and in what order)

Most CVs work best with these sections, top to bottom:

1. **Contact details** — so employers can reach you.
2. **Professional summary** — a short pitch of who you are.
3. **Work experience** — your jobs and what you achieved.
4. **Education** — degrees, courses, and training.
5. **Skills** — tools and abilities relevant to the role.
6. **Extras** — certifications, projects, languages, or volunteering.

Not sure whether you need a CV or a resume? They overlap a lot; here is a quick guide to [CV vs resume](/blog/cv-vs-resume) to help you decide.

## Contact details

Keep this small and clean. Include your full name, phone number, professional email, city and country, and a link to your LinkedIn or portfolio if you have one.

Skip your full home address, date of birth, and photo unless the job or country specifically expects them. They take up space and can invite bias.

## The professional summary

This is two to three sentences at the top of your CV. Its job is to tell the reader who you are, what you do well, and what you are aiming for. Write it last, once the rest of your CV is done, so you know what to highlight.

Here is the difference a focused summary makes:

> **Before:** "Hardworking person looking for a new opportunity where I can grow and use my skills."

> **After:** "Customer support specialist with 4 years helping SaaS users. Cut average response time by 30% and trained 6 new agents. Now seeking a team-lead role."

The "after" version names a role, a number, and a goal. That is what makes it land.

## Work experience

This is the heart of your CV. List jobs newest first. For each one, add your job title, the company, the location, and the dates.

Under each job, write three to six bullet points. The trick is to turn **duties** (what you were supposed to do) into **achievements** (what actually happened, ideally with a number).

A simple formula for each bullet:

- Start with a strong action verb (led, built, cut, grew, launched).
- Say what you did.
- Add a result or number where you can.

Watch a plain duty become an achievement:

> **Before:** "Responsible for managing the company's social media accounts."

> **After:** "Grew Instagram following from 2,000 to 11,000 in 8 months by posting daily and running 3 campaigns."

You will not always have exact numbers, and that is fine. Estimates ("about 20 orders a day"), percentages, and time saved all count. If you are stuck, CVify AI can rewrite a flat bullet into an achievement-style one and prompt you for the missing detail.

## Education

List your most recent or highest qualification first. Include the degree or course name, the school, and the year you finished (or "expected 2027" if you are still studying).

If you left school a while ago and have solid work experience, keep this short. New graduates can add more here: relevant modules, a strong grade, a thesis topic, or an academic award.

## Skills

Give a short, scannable list of skills that match the job. Mix the practical tools (software, languages, methods) with a few genuine strengths. For example:

- Excel, SQL, Google Analytics
- Project planning and budgeting
- Written and spoken French

Be honest. Only list skills you could talk about in an interview. CVify AI can suggest relevant skills based on your experience and the job you are targeting, which helps you spot gaps.

## Formatting and length

A CV should be easy to scan in a few seconds. Keep it simple:

- **Length:** one page for most people; two pages if you have many years of relevant experience. More than that is rarely needed.
- **Font:** one clean, readable font at a sensible size. No decorative fonts.
- **Layout:** clear headings, consistent spacing, and plenty of white space.
- **File type:** send a PDF unless the employer asks otherwise, so your layout stays put.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Typos and grammar slips — always proofread, then read it aloud.
- Listing duties instead of achievements.
- A generic CV sent to every job with no changes.
- Vague buzzwords ("team player", "go-getter") with nothing to back them up.
- Long blocks of text instead of short bullets.
- An unprofessional email address.
- Going over two pages, or shrinking the font to squeeze it in.

## Tailoring your CV to the job

Sending the same CV everywhere is the most common reason good candidates get ignored. A tailored CV mirrors the job you are applying for.

Here is how to tailor quickly:

- Read the job description and note the words and skills it repeats.
- Match those to your own experience, and move your most relevant points to the top.
- Use the same plain terms the employer uses (if they say "customer success", do not only say "client support").
- Adjust your summary to speak to that specific role.
