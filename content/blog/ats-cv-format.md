Before a human ever reads your CV, a piece of software often reads it first. That software is called an ATS, and if it cannot read your file properly, your application can get filtered out. The good news: the right ATS CV format is simple, and this guide walks you through it step by step.

## What Is an ATS (and Why Format Matters)

An ATS, or applicant tracking system, is software that companies use to collect and sort job applications. When you upload your CV, the ATS scans it, pulls out details like your name, job titles, skills, and dates, and stores them in a database. Recruiters then search that database for the right people.

Here is the key point: the ATS has to "read" your CV correctly. It reads the actual text in your file, one section at a time. If your layout is confusing, the software can jumble your details or miss them completely. That is why an ATS-friendly CV is not about fancy design. It is about clean, readable structure.

## The Rules of an ATS-Friendly CV

Follow these rules and most systems will read your CV cleanly:

- **Use a single-column layout.** One column, top to bottom. The ATS reads in a straight line, so a single column keeps everything in order.
- **Use standard section headings.** Stick to plain names like "Work Experience," "Education," "Skills," and "Summary." The software looks for these words to know what each part is.
- **Use standard fonts.** Common fonts like Arial, Calibri, Times New Roman, or Georgia are safe. Decorative fonts can confuse the reader.
- **Keep all text as real text.** The words must be selectable text, not part of a picture. If you cannot highlight a word with your mouse, the ATS probably cannot read it.
- **Use simple bullet points.** A plain round or square bullet works best. Skip unusual symbols or emojis in your bullet lists.
- **Use standard date formats.** Write dates in a clear format like "Jan 2024 - Mar 2026" or "2024 - 2026." Be consistent across the whole document.
- **Keep contact details in the body.** Put your name, email, and phone in the main body of the CV, not tucked into a design element.

## What Breaks ATS Parsing (The Avoid List)

Many CVs fail not because of the words, but because of the design. These common elements can break parsing, so it is safest to avoid them:

- **Tables.** The ATS can read a table in the wrong order or merge cells together, scrambling your details.
- **Text boxes.** Text trapped in a floating box is often skipped entirely.
- **Multiple columns.** Two or three columns can be read left-to-right across the page, mixing unrelated lines together.
- **Headers and footers with key info.** Some systems ignore the header and footer areas, so never put your name, email, or phone only up there.
- **Icons or graphics used as text.** A small phone or email icon is a picture, not text. The ATS reads nothing from it.
- **Images that contain words.** If your job title or skills sit inside a graphic, the software cannot pull them out.
- **Image-only PDFs.** A CV that is really just a scanned picture has no readable text at all (more on this next).

## File Format: Why a Real Text-Based PDF Wins

Most job sites accept PDF files, and a PDF is a great choice, but only the right kind of PDF.

There are two kinds:

- **A text-based PDF.** This is made from real, digital text. You can click and drag to select the words, and you can copy and paste them. This is exactly what an ATS needs, because the text is right there to be read.
- **An image-based PDF.** This is really a picture of a CV, often from a scan or a screenshot. It looks fine to your eyes, but there is no real text inside, so the software reads nothing.

Quick test: open your PDF and try to highlight a sentence with your mouse. If the words highlight, you have a text-based PDF. If nothing selects, or the whole page highlights as one block, it is an image, and an ATS will likely reject it.

Always export a genuine text-based PDF, and avoid scanning a printed copy or saving a design as a flat image.

## How to Include Keywords From the Job Description

An ATS often ranks CVs by how well they match the job description. That means the words you use matter.

Here is how to add keywords the natural way:

- Read the job posting and note the exact skills, tools, and job titles it mentions.
- Use those same words in your CV where they are true for you. If they ask for "project management" and you have done it, write "project management," not a clever synonym.
- Place keywords inside your real experience, like your bullet points and skills section, so they read naturally.
- Never paste a hidden list of keywords or repeat words over and over. This is called keyword stuffing, and it reads badly to the humans who see your CV next.

The goal is honest matching: use the employer's language to describe the real work you have done.

## Your ATS-Friendly CV Checklist

Before you send your CV, run through this quick list:

- Single-column layout, top to bottom
- Standard section headings (Work Experience, Education, Skills)
- A common, easy-to-read font
- All text is real, selectable text, not inside images
- Simple bullet points
- Clear, consistent date formats
- No tables, text boxes, or multiple columns
- Contact details in the main body, not only the header or footer
- Keywords from the job description used naturally
- Saved and exported as a text-based PDF

If you can tick every box, your CV is in strong shape for most systems.
